Repetitive Member Factor (\(C_r\))

The tabulated bending design values for dimension lumber 2” to 4” thick are multiplied by the repetitive member factor, Cr, which equals 1.15, when they are used as joists, truss, chords, rafters, studs, planks, decking, or similar applications. They must also:
  • Be in contact or spaced on 2’ centers or less
  • Consist of at least three members
  • Be joined by a floor, roof, or other load distributing element properly designed to support the loading condition.

Flat Use Factor (\(C_u\))

  • The Flat Use Factor could be used when analyzing built-up shapes.
  • o This is used when the load is applied to the wide face of the member.

Beam Stability Factor (\(C_L\))

  • Factor to ensure that a member is adequately braced to prevent buckling.
  • Shapes Closest to Square have a \(C_L\) == 0
  • The following tests are used to determine how the beam should be braced to reach a value of \(C_L\)==0
  • The NDS defines a method for determining \(C_L\). Use the following standards to determine when a beam is safe to use \(C_L\) == 0.
  • How to Determine \(C_L\):
    1. d/b ≤ 2: No lateral support shall be required
    2. 2 < d/b ≤ 4: The ends shall be held in position, as by full depth solid blocking, bridging, hangers, nailing or bolting to other framing members or other acceptable means.
    3. 4 < b/d ≤ 5: The compression edge of the member shall be held in line for its entire length to prevent lateral displacement, as by adequate sheathing or subflooring, and ends at point of bearing shall be held in position to prevent rotation and/or lateral displacement.
    4. 5 < b/d ≤ 6: Bridging, full depth solid blocking or diagonal cross bracing shall be installed at intervals not exceeding 8 feet, the compression edge of the member shall be held in line by adequate sheathing or subflooring, and the ends at points of bearing shall be held in position to prevent rotation and/or lateral displacement.
    5. 6 < d/b ≤ 7: Both edges shall be held in line for their entire length and ends at point of bearing shall be held in position to prevent rotation and/or lateral displacement.

Shear Test is the second step in the sawn lumber beam design process.

  • Shear occurs when one plane of a member slides past an adjacent plane.
  • Shear can occur horizontally, vertically, and diagonally.
  • WOOD IS MOST VULNERABLE TO HORIZONTAL SHEAR FORCES DUE TO WOOD GRAIN
  • Wood only shears in the vertical direction in extreme cases

Derivation of the Shear Formula

Horizontal Shear = \(V_{max}\)(Maximum Vertical Shear) along the beam $$ f_v=\ \frac{VQ}{Ib} $$
Where:
\(F_v\), The actual horizontal shear stress, (psi)
V, the magnitude of vertical shear at a location along the beam, (lbs)
Q, the Statical moment of area above or below the plane, (in\(^3\)
I, the moment of inertia in the plane of bending, (in\(^4\)
b, the width of the material at the plane, (in)
"Though the maximum horizontal shear stress of any beam occurs at the neutral axis, a structural designer can calculate the horizontal shear stress in other critical planes, such as a joint of a built-up shape. " -pg.95 To find \(f_v\) you must first find the statical moment of area (Q): Statical Moment of Area (Q):The area above or below the shear plane multiplied between the neutral axis of that area and the neutral axis of the shape.
  • Q is taken with respect of the neutral axis
  • For Convenience Q should be calculated for whichever area above or below the shear plane that is the least complex mathematically to solve for.
  • If the member is a complicated shape the structural designer can either:
    • A) find the statical moment of each component of the complex area and add them together to find Q
    • B) First find the neutral axis of the complex area then find the statical moment of the area for a complex area.
$$ Q=d_AA_A+d_BA_B+\ldots $$
Where:
Q, statical moment above or below the shear plane, (in\(^3\)
d, is the \(\perp\) distance between the neutral axis of a component and the neutral axis of the entire shape, (in)
A, the area of a component, (in\(^2\)
In a simple shape we can derive the equation into a simpler form. Looking at pg 96 we can calculate Q and \(f_v\) for solid rectangle. $$ Q_{rect}=\frac{{bd}^2}{8} $$ $$f_v=1.5\frac{V}{bd}\ or\ f_v=1.5\frac{V}{A}$$

Deflection Test

  • The deflection test is not a failure test
  • It is a test to see if a beam should be allowed for a given application
  • STANDARD DEFLECTION CRITERIA IS l/240
$$ \delta_{all}≤l360 or \delta_{all}≤l240 or \delta_{all}≤l180 $$
Where:
\delta_{all}, maximum allowable deflection, (in)
\(l\), length of the span, (in)

  • l/360 is the maximum amount of deflection for a live load such that a plaster ceiling would not crack over time.
  • l/240 is the criteria used for live loads that do not support a plaster ceiling
  • l/180 is the least restrictive criteria and is not often used, the beam will feel uncomfortably springy.

  1. Draw Free Body Diagram (FBD), indicating all loads including estimated beam weight.
  2. Determine \(V_{max}\) and \(M_{max}\) using case formulas or by finding reactions, where vertical shear crosses zero, and finding \(V_{max}\) and \(M_{max}\).
  3. Identify the applicable reference design values of \(F_b\), \(F_y\), and E for the specific species and Grad of Lumber used in the loading condition.
  4. Bending Test:
    1. Determine the applicable adjustment factor for the application (Refer to CH5.1 to determine the adjustment factors to use
      1. \(C_D\), Load Duration Factor(Normal == 10 Years or Less == 1.0, 2 Months == 1.15
      2. \(C_F\), Size Factor, must be estimated. A good rule of thumb is to start that every foot of span requires 1 inch of depth for a 2x member
        • UNLESS IT IS SOUTHERN PINE, OMIT CF IF BEAM IS MADE OF SOUTHERN PINE
      3. \(C_r\), Repetitive Member Factor, either 1.0 or 1.15.
    2. Calculate the adjusted bending design value, \(F_b\)
      3. $$ {f\prime}_b = (C_D\cdot C_F \cdot C_r)F_b $$
    3. Using \({f\prime}_b\) and \(M_{max}\) solve the flexure formula for the minimum required section modulus, \(S_{req}\)
      4. $$ S_{req}=\ \frac{M_{max}}{F_b^\prime} $$
    4. Using the Section Properties table (Appendix C, pg.467), find a beam which satisfies \(S_{req}\). If the beam is not in the estimated size range, repeat steps 1 through 3 above.
    5. Now that the actual beam is known, \(M_{max}\), \(S_{req}\), and \(F_b) may need to be recalculated if the weight of a beam exceeds the estimated beam weight. Beam weights are found in the section properties table in Appendix C
  5. Shear Test:
    1. Calculate the adjusted horizontal shear design value
      2. $$ {F\prime}_v = C_D \cdot F_b $$
    2. Use the horizontal shear formula for a solid rectangular section to find the actual horizontal shear stress
    3. Check that the beam selected for bending meets the minimum required area.
  6. Deflection Test:
    1. Choose the design criteria if not given in the problem. The standard for theatre is l/240.
      2. $$\delta_{all}≤l360 or \delta_{all}≤l240 or \delta_{all}≤l180$$
    2. Use a deflection formula if it is a simple beam.
    3. If the loading condition is atypical solve for \(w_{equiv}\) and then plug in to find the deflection (pg. 99 has an example
  7. Select a Beam
    1. Select a beam that passes all of the tests previously

Rule of Thumb

  • A long unsupported span will most likely fail due to the bending or deflection test
  • A short beam is more likely to fail due to the shear test

ADD PHOTO of THE CENTROID Notching a Beam: The process of cutting a slot out of a beam. Note: Notching a beam reduces the integrity of a beam
  • NDS recommends avoiding notching at any time however, the flexural properties of a beam will be unaffected if the following conditions are met:
    • Notches are not permitted in the middle ofa span
    • No notch length can be longer than 1/3 of the actual beam depth
    • Notch Height can not be taller than 1/4 of the beam height.
    • For notches in the end-thirds, the depth cannot be longer than 1/6 of the actual beam depth except for those beams directly over a support
The allowable shear stress parallel to the grain for a member notched at the ends can be calculated using the following equation: $$ V_{all}=(\frac{2{F^\prime}_vbd_n}{3}){(\frac{d_n}{d})}^2 $$
Where:
\({F\prime}_v), adjusted horizontal shear design value, (psi)
b, width of the member, (in)
d, depth of the member, (in)
\(d_n\), depth of the member minus the notch depth (in)
\(V_{all}\), depth of the member minus the notch depth (in)
