Statical Moment: The perpendicular distance from the centroid of a shape to a reference axis multiplied by the area of the shape.
$$ statical\space moment = d\perp \cdot A $$


Where:
d \(\perp\), is the distance from the neutral axis to the reference axis, (in.)
A, is the area of the shape, (in\(^2\))
Axis of the Plane of Bending: The axis perpendicular to the load, which is resisting the load, is acting against it.

​
Extreme Fiber Distance "c": Where the most stress will occur in a beam. The farthest fibers from the neutral axis where the most stress will occur.
  • For symmetrical shapes “c” is the same perpendicular distance (to the load) to either edge of the shape.
  • For asymmetrical shapes, it is important to differentiate which side “c” is measured to.


Centroid: Every beam has an infinite number of neutral axes, one for every possible loading angle, and every neutral axis of a shape will cross through a point known as the centroid.
  • The centroid is the center of gravity of the beams cross section; for a symmetrical object it is the center of the shape.

Where:
d \(\perp\), is the distance from the neutral axis to the reference axis, (in.)

Moment of Inertia (I, in\(^4\): Quantifies a beams resistance to deformation (deflection).

  • - Higher moment of inertia the less deflection will occur.

For infinitessimally small area: $$ I_\infty\ =\ Az^2 $$
Where:
A, is the area of the shape, (in\(^2\))
z, is the perpendicular distance from the neutral axis of a component to the neutral axis of the built-up shape, (in)
For non-infinitessimally small area: $$ I=\sum_{n=1}^{n=\infty}{A_nz_n^2} $$
  • This means that we will take an infinite number of areas in a shape to calculate the Moment of Inertia.
  • This will not be how we solve for moment of inertia, generally

STRUCTURES CONVENTION: For most scenarios we will use the equations in Appendix A for each specific shape. or Appendix C or D for specific lumber or metal shapes.
STRUCTURES CONVENTION: Moment of Inertia is additive if they share the same neutral axis.

For infinitessimally small area: $$ I_{tot}\ =\ \sum_{n=1}^{n=\infty}{I_{O_n}+A_nz_n^2} $$


Where:
\(I_{tot}\),moment of inertia of the whole shape, (in\(^4\))
\(I_{O_n}\), the original I value of a component, (in\(^4\))
\(A_n\), is the area of the shape, (in\(^2\))
\(z_n\), the perpendicular distance between the neutral axis of a component and the neutral axis of the whole shape, (in)

Steps to find the moment of Inertia for a built-up shape:

  1. Locate Neutral Axis of the built-up shape that is perpendicular to the load.
  2. Look up or calculate the moment of inertia, Ion, in the axis perpendicular to the load for each component.
  3. Use the equation above for each component to add up to the total Moment of Inertia, \(I_{tot}\)
Example If you have four components in a shape this is what the equation would look like:
\(I_{tot} =\) \({(I}_{o_1}+A_1z_1^2)+\)\({(I}_{o_2}+A_2z_2^2)+\)\({(I}_{o_3}+A_3z_3^2)+\)\({(I}_{o_1}+A_4z_4^2)\)

Radius of Gyration, r: Geometric Property that defines the resistance of a column to buckling.

  • The radius of gyration relates the moment of inertia and the cross-sectional area of a column:
$$ r=\sqrt{\frac{I}{A}} $$
Where:
r, radius of gyration, (in)
I, moment of inertia, (in\(^4\))
A, area, (in\(^2\)

Elastic Section Modulus, S: Predicts the capacity of a beam made from an elastic material to resist bending forces and is relative to both the size and shape of a section.
  • Unlike I, S is not additive unless the components of the built-up shape share the same neutral axis and the same extreme fiber distance.
$$ S=\ \frac{I}{c} $$
Where:
S, Elastic Section Modulus, (in\(^3\))
I, Moment of Inertia, (in\(^4\))
c, Extreme Fiber Distance, (in)

Flexure Formula: Quantifies the relationship of a beam to resist bending forces in regards to geometric properties and material choice.
$$ M=F_bS $$


Where:
M, bending moment, (inlb)
S, Elastic Section Modulus, (in\(^3\))
\(F_b\), allowable bending stress, (psi)

“A beam with a greater depth resists bending better than a shallow beam because of the greater distance between the neutral axis and the compressive and tensile stresses in the beam, which gives it a greater resistance to the bending forces” – pg76
- Illustrates why a shape is stronger when the depth (d) is larger than the base(b)

Plastic Analysis (pg. 80): For a metal beam: as bending moment increases, the bending stress in the extreme fibers increases until it reaches the allowable bending stress, Fb. As the bending moment increases the bending stress in the extreme fibers increases until it reaches the minimum yield stress, known as the elastic limit (\(F_y\)
Yield Moment (\(M_y\)): The moment that will produce elastic Limit(\(F_y\)) in the extreme fibers, \(M_y = F_yS\).
Plastic Stress Distribution: The point at which all of the fibers of a cross-section have reached (\(F_y\)).
Plastic Moment: The moment that will produce a full plastic stress distribution.
$$ M_P=\ F_YZ $$
Where:
\(M_P\), Plastic Moment, (inlb)
Z, Plastic Section Modulus, (in\(^3\))
\(F_y\), plastic limit, (psi)
